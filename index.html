Tastewise

The design journey of a cross-domain recommendation app — each version explores a different direction.

  1. Version 1 Clean & Minimal

    A calm, light design inspired by wellness and productivity apps. Lots of whitespace, a muted sage-green accent, and simple recommendation cards.
  2. Version 2 Dark & Editorial

    A high-contrast, magazine-inspired dark design. Bold serif typography, sharp lines, a red accent, and numbered editorial-style cards.
  3. Version 3 Brutalist Manifesto

    Raw, aggressive, intentionally ugly-beautiful. Monospaced type, thick borders, black and yellow, color-inversion hover states.
  4. Version 4 Retro 90s Web

    GeoCities nostalgia — Comic Sans, beveled borders, clashing colors, a scrolling marquee, visitor counter, and "Under Construction" energy.
  5. Version 5 Luxury Fashion

    Whisper-quiet elegance inspired by Celine and Aesop. Thin serif type, hairline rules, cream palette, and massive negative space.
  6. Version 6 Playful Kids App

    Bright, bubbly, Duolingo-inspired. Chunky fonts, rotated cards, thick colored borders, bold shadows, and pure joy.
  7. Version 7 Corporate SaaS

    Enterprise polish — blue-purple gradient hero, nav bar with CTAs, trust badges, and a clean professional grid layout.
  8. Version 8 Japanese Minimalism

    Wabi-sabi restraint — warm off-white, delicate typography, asymmetric layout, thin rules, and a vertical Japanese text accent.
  9. Version 9 Neon Cyberpunk

    Blade Runner meets synthwave. Deep purple-black, neon glow effects in pink/cyan/green, scanlines, and glowing card borders.
  10. Version 10 Indie Zine

    Photocopied DIY aesthetic — handwriting font, dashed borders, tilted cards, red stamp accents, and scissors-cut-here footer.
  11. Version 11 Travel Magazine

    Condé Nast warmth — cream and gold palette, serif masthead, editorial magazine grid, and premium print typography.
  12. Version 12 Vaporwave

    Dreamy retrowave — pink-to-purple gradients, perspective grid lines, pastel neon palette, and 80s mall surrealism.
  13. Version 13 Newspaper Broadsheet

    Classic front page — all-serif, strict black and white, multi-column grid, justified text, and a classified-ad search box.
  14. Version 14 Botanical / Nature

    Earthy field guide aesthetic — forest green and terracotta on cream, serif type, and decorative botanical corner ornaments.
  15. Version 15 Sci-Fi Terminal

    1980s mainframe — black screen, green monospace text, scanlines, blinking cursor, ASCII box-drawing borders, and CRT glow.
Designer's note After exploring 15 radically different directions, I noticed I was drawn to designs that felt serious and considered — editorial, authoritative, a little like a culture magazine or broadsheet. The playful versions felt too flippant for an app about taste. Going forward I want to explore layouts that break the standard input-on-top, three-cards-below structure, and designs that give more context about why a recommendation is being made — not just what it is.
  1. Version 16 Accordion Editorial

    Long-form single-column editorial with click-to-expand accordion panels (CSS checkbox hack). Search embedded mid-page like a pull quote.
  2. Version 17 Split Screen

    Fixed dark left panel with branding and search, scrollable cream right panel with full-width editorial recommendation sections.
  3. Version 18 Hover-Reveal Triptych

    Three full-height panels that expand on hover to reveal recommendations. Flex-based interaction with dark background transitions and fade-in text.
  4. Version 19 Scroll Narrative

    Six full-viewport sections with CSS scroll-snap — a page-turning editorial experience with alternating dark and light backgrounds.
  5. Version 20 Tabbed Single-Focus

    One recommendation at a time, controlled by elegant pill tabs using the CSS radio button hack. Full-viewport panels with crossfade transitions.
Designer's note After exploring layouts and interactions in v16–v20, v19 emerged as a clear favorite. The scroll-snap narrative structure gives each recommendation its own moment — it creates a sense of ceremony that matches what Tastewise is about. Taking taste seriously means giving each suggestion space to breathe. I'm keeping the color palette, which I found to be serious, yet pleasant and tasteful. For v21–v25, I'm narrowing my focus to typography — exploring how different type choices shift the feeling from literary journal to culture magazine to refined editorial, all within the same layout.
  1. Version 21 Editorial Serif

    Playfair Display at black weight for headings, Georgia for body. High-contrast thick/thin strokes — literary journal masthead energy.
  2. Version 22 Clean Grotesque

    Inter throughout at light weights. Modern, understated, almost invisible — the type gets out of the way and lets the content breathe.
  3. Version 23 Serif + Grotesque

    DM Serif Display for headlines, DM Sans for body. A classic editorial pairing — confident serif headings with a clean sans companion.
  4. Version 24 Monospace Editorial

    IBM Plex Mono at light weights throughout. An unexpected choice that reads as intellectual and precise — like a beautifully typeset manuscript.
Designer's note After four typography experiments, v19 remains the strongest version. The process was clarifying: sans-serif and monospace fonts felt too cold and minimal for what Tastewise is — an app about human taste and personal connection. The heavier serifs explored in v21 and v23 swung too far the other way, feeling imposing rather than inviting. v19's original serif choices sit in the right place — warm and considered, but never overbearing.
